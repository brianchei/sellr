# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Sellr serves members of verified local communities who want peer-to-peer resale
to feel safer, easier, and more context-aware than a generic listing board.
Initial launch users are UW-Madison students in Badger Market, but the product
should stay general enough for campuses, coworking spaces, residential
communities, workplaces, and other invite- or domain-gated groups.

Core users:

- Buyers scanning nearby inventory, judging trust, and deciding whether to
  contact a seller.
- Sellers creating listings, managing inventory, responding to buyers, and
  marking items sold.
- Community admins reviewing reports, managing access, and preserving local
  trust boundaries.

Users are usually in a task: browse, sell, reply, report, moderate, or prepare
for pickup. Product UI should make those tasks fast, legible, and confidence
building.

## Product Purpose

Sellr is a trust-native local marketplace for verified communities. It exists to
make local resale feel complete from community access through listing, buyer
contact, messaging, pickup coordination, reporting, and basic admin moderation.

Success means:

- A new member can understand why the community is trusted and reach the next
  action quickly.
- Buyers can compare items, seller context, pickup area, and trust cues without
  wading through explanation.
- Sellers can publish complete listings with photos and useful pickup
  information.
- Admins can inspect reports and act without ambiguity.
- The product feels more polished and trustworthy than a broad marketplace,
  while staying smaller and clearer than a heavyweight commerce platform.

## Brand Personality

Sellr should feel clear, local, structured, warm, and trustworthy. It should
have enough personality to feel like a real community marketplace, but the app
should never become decorative at the cost of scanning, trust, or task speed.

The strongest emotional targets are confidence, neighborly familiarity, and
practical momentum. The product voice should be direct and calm. Use short
verbs, concrete trust language, and honest explanations only when they help the
current decision.

## Anti-references

Avoid:

- Generic AI product styling, cinematic dark onboarding, glow-heavy surfaces,
  and vague "smart marketplace" mood.
- Decorative dashboard composition on signed-in surfaces.
- Heavy nested cards, frosted glass, bokeh blobs, gradient text, and generic
  hero metric blocks.
- Fake launch proof, invented testimonials, inflated safety claims, or trust
  language not backed by the product.
- Broad marketplace feature creep such as payments, ratings, delivery,
  logistics, advanced reputation, growth loops, or broad admin tooling before
  launch.
- A new design-system library for the current web SLC.

## Design Principles

1. Trust is contextual. Put community, seller, pickup, report, and verification
   cues next to the listing, profile, conversation, or admin action they affect.
2. The item comes first. Marketplace surfaces should prioritize photo, price,
   title, condition, pickup area, seller, and availability before explanation.
3. Operational screens stay calm. Seller, inbox, notification, profile, and
   admin screens should use rows, dividers, compact panels, and clear labels
   before decorative cards.
4. The launch scope is complete before it is broad. Preserve auth, onboarding,
   community scoping, listing lifecycle, media upload, messaging, reporting,
   admin, and readiness gates.
5. Inspiration is translated, not copied. Reference sources should become
   Sellr-specific decisions about hierarchy, density, trust, motion, and
   responsiveness.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Design and implementation should meet a practical WCAG AA bar for core flows.
Every changed interactive flow should preserve loading, empty, error,
validation, disabled, focus, and success states.

Important checks:

- Visible focus states for keyboard users.
- Field errors associated with relevant inputs when possible.
- No color-only status communication.
- No clipped text, overlapping content, or horizontal overflow on mobile.
- Stable action sizing for buttons, rows, cards, filters, and message inputs.
- Reduced motion compatible interactions; motion should communicate state, not
  decorate the page.
